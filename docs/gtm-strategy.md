## 1. Executive Summary

Gneva is an AI team member product that joins meetings, builds persistent organizational memory, and evolves from a silent observer into an active voice participant and project manager surrogate. Unlike meeting transcription tools that produce forgettable summaries, Gneva accumulates institutional knowledge across every call, thread, and decision, making it genuinely valuable to the organization over time — not just the individual who set it up.

The core thesis: organizations don't need better notes. They need a team member who was in every room, remembers every commitment, and can be asked anything at any time. That product does not exist. Gneva builds it.

**Strategic anchors:**

- **Network-locked memory**: Gneva becomes more valuable with every meeting it attends. Switching costs compound monthly.
- **Bottom-up enterprise sales**: Individuals adopt it free, teams upgrade for shared memory, companies pay for voice and compliance.
- **Solo-bootstrappable**: Break-even at ~50 Pro users. $80K MRR achievable inside 12 months with disciplined execution.

**Year 1 financial snapshot:**

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Break-even (months) | ~4 |
| Year 1 net income | ~$128K |
| Target MRR at month 12 | $80K+ |
| CAC (self-serve) | <$100 |
| LTV target | $1,200+ |

## 2. Market Sizing

### 2.1 Total Addressable Market (TAM)

The TAM is defined as all workplace AI spending, encompassing meeting intelligence, knowledge management, project management AI, and enterprise virtual assistants.

- **Workplace AI market (2025):** $42B globally
- **CAGR:** 35–43% through 2030
- **Projected TAM (2030):** $180–220B

This broad framing is justified because Gneva eventually competes not just with meeting tools but with internal wikis, project management software, onboarding tools, and executive assistants. The value proposition expands as the product matures.

### 2.2 Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM)

Narrowing to organizations actively using video conferencing and seeking to improve meeting ROI and institutional knowledge retention:

- **AI meeting assistant market (2025):** $3–3.5B
- **AI agents market (2025):** $5.1B, growing at 43.3% CAGR
- **Combined SAM (meeting intelligence + agentic work):** ~$8–10B in 2025

**Customer profile for SAM:**
- Companies with 10–5,000 employees
- Remote-first or hybrid work model
- Active use of Zoom, Google Meet, or Microsoft Teams
- Existing spend on productivity or knowledge management tools

Estimated 4.2 million such organizations globally. At $500 average revenue per organization per year (blended), SAM ≈ $2.1B annually today.

### 2.3 Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM)

Realistic 3-year capture, assuming:
- Strong product-market fit in the 10–200 employee segment
- Bottom-up growth via individual Pro users converting to Team plans
- Geographic focus on English-speaking markets (US, UK, Canada, Australia) in Year 1

| Horizon | Users | ARR Target | SAM Share |
|---|---|---|---|
| Year 1 | ~2,500 paying | ~$1.1M | 0.05% |
| Year 2 | ~12,000 paying | ~$6.2M | 0.30% |
| Year 3 | ~40,000 paying | ~$22M | 1.05% |

SOM is intentionally conservative. A 1% SAM capture in 3 years is achievable for a solo-founded product with distribution advantages; exceeding it depends on viral loops and enterprise motion maturing earlier than planned.

### 2.4 Market Tailwinds

- **Remote/hybrid normalization**: 58% of US knowledge workers now work remotely at least part-time. Meeting volume has grown 3× since 2019. More meetings = more need for memory.
- **AI agent adoption curve**: Organizations moved from AI curiosity to AI budgets in 2025. Meeting intelligence is a low-risk entry point — easy to trial, easy to measure value.
- **Knowledge debt crisis**: The average Fortune 500 loses $31.5B per year to employees failing to share knowledge effectively (IDC). Gneva attacks this directly.
- **The "meeting tax" narrative**: Executives widely accept that meetings are expensive and under-leveraged. Gneva has a simple ROI story: a $49/user/month tool that eliminates two wasted hours of meeting recovery per week pays for itself 10× over.

## 3. Competitive Landscape & Moat Analysis

### 3.1 Competitive Map

The competitive space is segmented along two axes: **scope** (individual vs. organizational) and **participation** (passive observer vs. active voice).

```
                        ACTIVE VOICE
                             |
              Gneva Enterprise |
                             |
     ________________________|________________________
    |                        |                        |
INDIVIDUAL                   |                   ORGANIZATIONAL
    |                        |                        |
    |   Otter.ai             |         Read.ai        |
    |   Fireflies            |         Notion AI       |
    |   Fathom               |         Confluence AI   |
    |________________________|________________________|
                             |
                        PASSIVE OBSERVER
```

**Gneva's unique position:** The only product targeting the upper-right quadrant — organizational scope with active voice participation. No competitor currently occupies this space. This is the moat.

### 3.2 Competitor Profiles

**Otter.ai**
- Strengths: Brand recognition, established user base, real-time transcription
- Weaknesses: Individual-focused, no persistent org memory, no voice participation, commodity transcription race
- Threat level: Low. Customers already know the need; Otter proves it but doesn't fill it.

**Fireflies.ai**
- Strengths: Good integrations, reasonable pricing, CRM sync
- Weaknesses: Summary-focused (not memory-focused), no cross-meeting intelligence, passive only
- Threat level: Low-Medium. Similar motion to Gneva's early stages; could copy.

**Fathom**
- Strengths: Free tier, Zoom-native, excellent UX for summaries
- Weaknesses: No memory layer, no org features, no cross-meeting reasoning
- Threat level: Low. Positioned as "better notes," not "team member."

**Read.ai**
- Strengths: Cross-platform (Zoom + Teams + Meet), engagement analytics, some team features, active development
- Weaknesses: Individual analytics focus, no persistent org memory, no voice participation, no PM capabilities
- Threat level: **High**. Closest competitor in ambition. Best-funded in the space. Watch for voice feature announcements.

**Notion AI / Confluence AI**
- Strengths: Embedded in existing workflows, large installed bases
- Weaknesses: Passive documentation tools — they don't attend meetings, they wait for you to bring information to them
- Threat level: Medium. Could build meeting ingestion. If Notion adds "join meeting" functionality, it competes.

**Microsoft Copilot (Teams)**
- Strengths: Native Teams integration, enterprise distribution, Microsoft trust
- Weaknesses: Teams-only, individual summaries, no persistent memory across meetings, no voice, no PM agency
- Threat level: **Very High for Enterprise**. Microsoft will expand Copilot. Plan to win on memory depth and multi-platform before Microsoft catches up, then use that data moat as defense.

**Aisera / Moveworks**
- Enterprise IT/HR AI assistants. Different use case — not meeting-native.
- Threat level: Low.

### 3.3 Competitive Moat Analysis

Gneva's durable advantages layer over time:

**Layer 1: First-mover in the org-memory + voice quadrant (Months 1–12)**
No one is here yet. The first product to deliver reliable cross-meeting organizational memory with voice participation owns the narrative. Being first matters for SEO, press, and category definition.

**Layer 2: Data compounding moat (Months 6+)**
Each meeting Gneva attends adds to the org's memory graph. After 6 months of meetings, Gneva holds context no new entrant can replicate without starting over. Switching costs grow quadratically — not linearly — with meeting count.

**Layer 3: Network effects within teams (Months 6–12)**
When multiple team members use Gneva, it builds shared context. Individual users on a team who try to remove Gneva find that their colleagues' workflows depend on it. Team-level stickiness converts individual trials into locked accounts.

**Layer 4: Voice identity and relationship familiarity (Year 2+)**
When Gneva has attended 500 meetings for an organization, team members have a relationship with it. They know how it speaks. They reference it by name. Custom voice personas for Enterprise customers create lock-in that is genuinely irreplaceable — you can't export a voice relationship.

**Layer 5: PM workflow integration (Year 2+)**
Once Gneva is scheduling follow-ups, tracking action items, and reminding owners of commitments, it is woven into the operating rhythm of the company. At that stage, Gneva is not a tool — it is a team member, and team members are not unsubscribed.

### 3.4 Moat Vulnerability Assessment

| Risk | Likelihood | Severity | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Microsoft Copilot adds org memory | High | High | Win SMB first, use data depth as defense |
| Read.ai adds voice participation | Medium | High | Accelerate voice timeline to Year 1 Q4 |
| Notion launches meeting ingestion | Medium | Medium | Gneva is active participant; Notion is passive |
| Open-source transcription commoditizes base layer | High | Low | Moat is memory + voice, not transcription |
| Privacy regulation kills meeting recording | Low | Very High | Consent-first architecture; see Section 12 |

## 4. Product Strategy & Positioning

### 4.1 The Three Phases of Gneva

**Phase 1: Silent Observer (Months 1–6)**
Gneva joins meetings as a non-speaking participant. It transcribes, summarizes, extracts action items, and builds the organization's memory graph. Users interact with Gneva via @mentions in Slack or a web dashboard: "Gneva, what did we decide about the rebrand last month?" This phase proves the memory value proposition.

**Phase 2: Active Responder (Months 7–12)**
Gneva gains a voice. It can be addressed in meetings — "Gneva, what did we say about this in the Q4 planning call?" — and responds verbally. It can also speak up proactively when it detects a decision contradicting a prior commitment. This phase transforms Gneva from a tool into a team member.

**Phase 3: PM Surrogate (Year 2+)**
Gneva owns action items. It schedules follow-ups, pings owners when deadlines approach, resurfaces relevant context when recurring topics arise, and generates weekly team health digests. It attends stand-ups and reports on project status from memory. At this stage, Gneva is a junior project manager — the highest-value and highest-retention tier.

### 4.2 Positioning Statement

For teams of 5–500 who run on meetings and lose critical knowledge between them, Gneva is the AI team member that attends every meeting, never forgets a decision, and grows into an active voice in the room. Unlike Otter or Fireflies, which produce summaries for individuals, Gneva builds organizational memory that compounds over time and speaks when it matters.

### 4.3 The "New Team Member" Framing

The single most important positioning decision: Gneva is not a tool. It is a team member. This framing:

- Justifies per-seat pricing (you pay per team member, not per feature)
- Creates emotional adoption (you introduce Gneva; you don't configure it)
- Enables viral growth (new employees are "introduced to Gneva" during onboarding)
- Sets the right expectation for the memory moat (team members get smarter over time)

Every piece of copy, onboarding flow, and Slack message should reinforce this frame. Gneva sends messages in first person. Gneva has a name, not a logo. Gneva says "I remember" not "the transcript shows."

## 5. Pricing Strategy Deep Dive

### 5.1 Pricing Tiers

| Tier | Price | Target User | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Free** | $0 | Individual / trial | 5 meetings/month, basic summaries, 30-day memory |
| **Pro** | $29/month | Individual power user | Unlimited meetings, full memory, @Gneva chat, 3 integrations |
| **Team** | $49/user/month | Teams 5–50 | Org memory, team insights, Slack integration, admin dashboard |
| **Enterprise** | $79+/user/month | 50+ users | Voice participation, on-prem option, SSO/SAML, custom voice, SLA |

### 5.2 Pricing Philosophy

**Why $29 for Pro?**
- Below the psychological "$1/day" threshold ($30/month feels expensive; $29/month does not)
- Cheap enough for individuals to expense without approval
- High enough to signal this is a professional tool (below $20 reads as toy)
- Comparison anchor: one hour of an executive assistant's time

**Why $49/user for Team?**
- Priced as a "team member" not a "software license" — $49/month per person is the cost of a decent lunch
- Org memory feature creates genuine incremental value over Pro (not just volume)
- Team plans require minimum 3 seats, so minimum $147/month — meaningful MRR per account
- Upgrade path from Pro is obvious: "Your team is also using Gneva, but you're not sharing memory"

**Why $79+ for Enterprise?**
- Voice participation is the anchor feature — it has no comparable product to benchmark against
- On-prem and SSO are table-stakes for enterprise; they justify the premium
- Custom voice is a genuinely unique capability with no market comparison — price it high
- Enterprise floor of $79 with volume discounts; large deals negotiated at $55–65/user with annual commit

**Psychological Pricing Tactics**

- Annual billing discount: 2 months free (16.7% discount) — show monthly equivalent to anchor on lower number
- Free tier is genuinely useful (5 meetings/month = typical bi-weekly user), not crippled
- Team tier default to annual billing in checkout flow — reduces churn, improves cash flow
- Enterprise: quote-based with anchor at $79 seats; actual deal value usually $55–65/seat annual

### 5.3 Freemium Conversion Mechanics

The Free tier is the top of the funnel, not a retention tier. Conversion triggers are designed to hit at moments of genuine value realization:

1. **Memory limit hit**: Free users get 30-day memory. When they try to ask "what did we say about X in January?" and hit the wall, the upgrade prompt is perfectly timed.
2. **6th meeting of the month**: Free limit is 5. On the 6th invite, Gneva sends a personal message: "I'd love to join, but I've used all my free meetings this month. Want to keep me on the team?"
3. **Team invitation**: When a Free user invites Gneva to a meeting where others are present, it surfaces Team tier features post-meeting: "I noticed 4 of your colleagues were in that call. If you upgrade to Team, we can build shared memory across your organization."
4. **@Gneva query on paid feature**: Free users can see the @Gneva interface but get gated responses for cross-meeting queries: "I remember discussions about this from before last month — upgrade Pro to unlock full memory."

### 5.4 Enterprise Packaging

Enterprise is not just more features — it is a different procurement motion:

- **Procurement**: Legal review, security questionnaire, IT sign-off. Offer SOC 2 Type II report, BAA for healthcare, DPA for GDPR.
- **Champion program**: Identify the internal champion (usually a chief of staff, operations lead, or IT director). Give them a free Team account for 90 days with white-glove onboarding.
- **Pilot structure**: 30-day pilot with defined success metrics (reduction in "what did we decide?" Slack messages, action item completion rate, meeting prep time). Measure and share results.
- **Custom voice**: Charged as an add-on ($10–15/user/month on top of base Enterprise). Allows org to name their Gneva instance and choose voice characteristics. Massive lock-in — teams become attached.

### 6.2 Gross Margins by Tier

| Tier | Price | COGS | Gross Margin | Gross Margin % |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pro | $29 | $1.00 | $28.00 | **96.6%** |

### 6.3 CAC Model

**Self-serve (Free → Pro)**
- Primary channel: Content marketing + Product Hunt + community
- CAC target: <$100

**Team tier (individual → team upgrade or direct outbound)**
- Primary channel: Bottom-up (Free/Pro users inviting colleagues) + light outbound
- CAC target: <$500
- Breakdown: ~$150 sales touch (email sequence + demo), ~$200 trial support, ~$150 onboarding
- At scale: <$300 via PLG motion

**Enterprise (outbound + inbound from team accounts)**
- CAC target: <$2,000
- Breakdown: ~$500 outbound/SDR, ~$800 demo + POC support, ~$700 legal/procurement overhead
- Fully-loaded with founder time in Year 1: may reach $3,000–4,000 per Enterprise account; improve in Year 2 with hire

## 7. Customer Acquisition Strategy

### 7.2 Channel Strategy

#### Channel 1: Content Marketing (Primary, Months 1–12)

**Investment:** ~$500/month + 10 hrs/week founder time
**Target ROI:** 40% of total free signups

The meeting intelligence category has high SEO potential for informational and comparison queries. Produce content that ranks before Gneva launches and continues compounding.

**Target keyword clusters:**
- "best AI meeting assistant 2026" (high volume, commercial intent)
- "how to never lose meeting context" (problem-aware, top of funnel)
- "team knowledge management AI" (SAM targeting)
- "Otter vs Fireflies vs [Gneva]" (comparison, bottom funnel)
- "recording consent laws [jurisdiction]" (legal questions — high trust content)

**Content formats:**
- Long-form guides (2,500+ words): "The Complete Guide to AI Meeting Assistants in 2026"
- Comparison posts: "Otter.ai vs Fireflies vs Fathom vs Gneva — An Honest Comparison"
- Use-case posts: "How Remote Engineering Teams Use AI to Eliminate Redundant Meetings"
- Thought leadership: "Why Your Meeting Notes Are Killing Your Organization's Memory"
- Legal/compliance: "Recording Consent Laws for Remote Meetings: A State-by-State Guide"

**Distribution:** Publish → submit to Hacker News / Reddit / LinkedIn → repurpose as threads → email newsletter

#### Channel 2: Community & Developer Distribution (Primary, Months 1–6)

**Investment:** $0 cash, ~8 hrs/week
**Target ROI:** 30% of early adopters

The founding story — "the AI that remembers what your team forgot" — is inherently shareable. Identify and contribute genuinely to communities before launching.

**Target communities:**
- Hacker News: "Show HN" launch + "Ask HN: How do you handle organizational memory?" threads
- r/productivity, r/remotework, r/startup (genuine participation, not spam)
- Twitter/X: Build in public. Share metrics, learnings, interesting Gneva interactions.
- LinkedIn: Target operations leads, chiefs of staff, founders. These are Team/Enterprise buyers.
- Indie Hackers: Build-in-public series. This community converts well to early adopters.
- Product Hunt: Plan a coordinated launch in Month 3 to coincide with beta.

**Tactic: Build in public**
Share monthly revenue, user counts, and product decisions publicly. This generates press, community goodwill, and recruits early advocates who become case studies.

#### Channel 3: Product-Led Growth (Core Mechanic, Months 3+)

**Investment:** Engineering time, $0 cash
**Target ROI:** 25% of Team tier conversions

Every meeting Gneva attends is a distribution event. When Gneva joins a meeting with 8 attendees, 7 of those attendees see Gneva and potentially become users.

**PLG mechanics:**
- Gneva's calendar invite includes: "Introduced by [host]. Want your own Gneva? → link"
- Post-meeting summary emails include Gneva branding and signup prompt for non-users
- @Gneva Slack messages are visible to the whole channel — non-users see the product in action
- "Gneva is attending" notification in Zoom/Meet shows Gneva's profile with a link

**Virality coefficient target:** K > 0.3 (each user brings 0.3 additional users organically). Achievable via meeting invitations.

#### Channel 4: Partnerships (Months 6+)

**Investment:** Negotiation time, potential revenue share
**Target ROI:** 10–15% of Enterprise pipeline

**Priority partners:**
- **Notion**: Integration for piping meeting memory into Notion databases. Notion's marketplace has 6M+ users.
- **Linear / Jira**: Gneva creates tickets from action items. Engineering teams love this.
- **Zapier / Make**: Gneva as a Zapier app — connects to thousands of workflows
- **Slack**: Be featured in Slack App Directory. Requires app review but drives organic installs.
- **HubSpot / Salesforce**: Pipe meeting memory into CRM. Sales team use case is very strong.

#### Channel 5: Light Outbound (Months 7+)

**Investment:** $500/month (Apollo.io or Clay) + 5 hrs/week
**Target ROI:** 20% of Team tier new accounts

Target: Operations leads, chiefs of staff, remote-first companies, companies with distributed engineering teams.

**Sequence:**
1. Email 1: Share relevant content piece (recording consent guide, etc.). No pitch.
2. Email 2 (Day 5): Short case study. "Team X went from 3 hours of meeting recovery per week to 20 minutes."
3. Email 3 (Day 10): Direct ask. "Would a 20-minute demo make sense?"
4. LinkedIn connection + DM if no email response.

**Volume target:** 200 outbound/month, 5% demo rate = 10 demos/month, 30% close rate = 3 new accounts/month.

### 7.3 Launch Sequence

**Pre-launch (Months 1–2):**
- Build waitlist via "building in public" posts on Twitter/X and LinkedIn
- Target 500+ waitlist signups before beta
- Publish 3 long-form content pieces to begin ranking

## 12. Legal Considerations: Recording Consent by Jurisdiction

Recording consent law is the most significant legal risk for Gneva. This section must inform product architecture, not just legal disclaimers.

### 12.1 Core Legal Framework

Two principal frameworks govern recording consent:

**One-party consent:** Only one party to the conversation needs to consent. The recorder (or person authorizing the recording) satisfies this.

**Two-party (all-party) consent:** All parties to the conversation must consent before recording begins.

### 12.2 United States — State-by-State

**One-party consent states (majority):**
Federal law (Electronic Communications Privacy Act) requires one-party consent. Most US states follow federal minimum. These include: Texas, New York, Florida (in-state calls), Georgia, Colorado, Ohio, Illinois (with exceptions), and ~30 others.

**All-party / two-party consent states:**
| State | Law | Key Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| **California** | Penal Code §632 | All-party consent required. Violation: felony. Fine up to $2,500 per violation + civil liability. **Critical — largest SaaS market.** |
| **Florida** | §934.03 F.S. | All-party consent for in-state calls. Non-resident callers may not trigger this. Complex. |
| **Illinois** | 720 ILCS 5/14-2 | All-party consent in certain contexts. Court interpretation varies. |
| **Maryland** | Md. Code §10-402 | All-party consent. |
| **Massachusetts** | MGL c.272 §99 | All-party consent. Criminal penalties. |
| **Michigan** | MCL §750.539c | All-party consent. |
| **Montana** | §45-8-213 MCA | All-party consent. |
| **Gnevada** | NRS §200.620 | All-party consent. |
| **New Hampshire** | RSA §570-A:2 | All-party consent. |
| **Oregon** | ORS §165.540 | All-party consent. |
| **Pennsylvania** | 18 Pa.C.S. §5703 | All-party consent. Criminal penalties. |
| **Washington** | RCW §9.73.030 | All-party consent. Civil + criminal liability. |

**Practical implication for Gneva:** Because California alone accounts for ~30% of US B2B SaaS customers, Gneva **must** operate as if all-party consent is required in all meetings. Build accordingly.

### 12.3 International Jurisdictions

| Jurisdiction | Framework | Key Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| **European Union** | GDPR Article 6 + national law | Lawful basis required (consent or legitimate interest). Must inform participants of recording. Right to erasure applies to transcripts. |
| **United Kingdom** | UK GDPR + Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act | Similar to EU. Post-Brexit UK GDPR aligns closely with EU GDPR. |
| **Canada** | PIPEDA (federal) + provincial laws (Quebec Law 25 most strict) | Consent required for collection and use of personal information (voice = personal data). |
| **Australia** | Privacy Act 1988 + Telecommunications (Interception) Act | Notification required; consent for telephone calls in some states. |
| **Germany** | GDPR + §201 StGB | Extremely strict. Recording without explicit consent is a criminal offense. All-party consent mandatory. |
| **France** | GDPR + French Data Protection Law | Consent required. CNIL (regulator) has issued guidance on AI in meetings. |
| **Japan** | Act on Protection of Personal Information | Consent required for personal data collection; voice recordings qualify. |

### 12.4 Gneva's Consent Architecture (Product Requirements)

**Non-negotiable technical requirements:**

1. **Consent announcement at meeting start**: When Gneva joins any meeting, it must audibly announce: "Hi, I'm Gneva, an AI assistant. This meeting will be recorded and transcribed for [Organization Name]. If you'd like to opt out, please let the host know or leave the meeting." This announcement is required, automatic, and cannot be disabled.

2. **Consent banner in meeting UI**: For platforms that support it (Zoom, Teams), a visible notification in the meeting UI indicating recording is in progress. Most platforms have native functionality for this.

3. **Meeting host consent**: The meeting host (Gneva's customer) must explicitly acknowledge consent obligations in Terms of Service during signup. They accept responsibility for obtaining participant consent in their jurisdiction.

4. **External participant handling**: Gneva must flag meetings with external participants (non-org email domains) for heightened consent treatment. Recommend Enterprise customers configure external participant notifications.

5. **Data retention controls**: GDPR requires data minimization and erasure rights. Provide:
   - Configurable retention period (30/90/365 days or custom)
   - "Forget this meeting" function (deletes transcript + memory entries from that meeting)
   - User data export (GDPR Article 20 portability right)
   - Account deletion with full data erasure within 30 days

6. **Geographic data residency (Enterprise)**: EU customers must be offered EU data residency (AWS eu-west-1 or equivalent). This is a hard requirement for German, French, and many other EU Enterprise customers.

7. **No recording of one-on-ones without both-party consent**: One-on-one meetings are higher risk than group meetings. Require explicit opt-in for 1:1 recording vs. default-on for group meetings.

### 12.5 Legal Setup Recommendations

- **Retain privacy counsel** familiar with GDPR and US state recording laws before launch. Budget: $3,000–5,000 for initial review + DPA template + terms of service.
- **SOC 2 Type II**: Begin audit preparation in Month 9. Required for Enterprise, expected by Team tier. Cost: $15,000–$30,000.
- **Data Processing Agreement (DPA)**: Required for all EU customers. Have template ready at Pro tier launch.
- **Business Associate Agreement (BAA)**: Required for healthcare customers (HIPAA). Have template ready before pursuing healthcare vertical.
- **Terms of Service**: Include recording consent acknowledgment, jurisdiction-specific provisions for CA/EU, and clear liability allocation for host responsibility.

## 13. Solo Founder Execution Guide

### 13.1 The Core Constraint

As a solo founder, your scarcest resource is not money — it is focused hours. Every hour spent on a task you don't need to do personally is an hour not spent on the three things only you can do: **product direction, early sales, and code** (until you hire).

### 13.2 Weekly Time Budget (Year 1)

| Activity | Hours/Week | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Core engineering | 30 | Protect this ruthlessly |
| Customer conversations | 5 | Talk to users every week. No exceptions. |
| Content / marketing | 5 | Batch: write one long piece per week |
| Operations / admin | 3 | Automate everything possible |
| Strategic thinking | 2 | Walks, journal, not at desk |
| **Total** | **45** | Sustainable for 12 months |

### 13.3 What to Outsource Immediately

From Day 1 cash, outsource tasks that consume hours but don't require your judgment:

- **Design**: Hire a freelance UI/UX designer (Toptal, Dribbble) for the landing page and dashboard. Budget $2,000–3,000 for a polished initial design. Don't do it yourself unless you're a strong designer.
- **Legal**: Use templates (Termly, Clerky) for initial terms; pay for one-time counsel review. Don't write these from scratch.
- **Accounting**: Use an automated bookkeeping service (Bench, Pilot) from Month 1. $300/month. Worth every dollar.
- **Customer support (Month 6+)**: Hire a part-time contractor for Tier 1 support (FAQ responses, billing questions). Frees 5+ hours/week.
- **Content amplification**: Use a VA to post and distribute content you've written. Don't write and distribute — delegate distribution.

### 13.4 No-Scope-Creep Rules

Define and defend scope with iron discipline. The graveyard of solo-founder SaaS is littered with products that tried to be everything for everyone in Year 1.

**Year 1 scope rules:**
- Only three integrations: Zoom, Google Meet, Slack. Everything else is Year 2.
- English-language only until Month 10. Internationalization is a Year 2 problem.
- No mobile app until there are 1,000 active users asking for it.
- No API access until Enterprise tier (Month 11).
- Resist custom feature requests until they appear 3+ times independently.

**The "customer asks for X" test:** Before building any customer-requested feature, ask:
1. Has at least 3 different customers asked for this independently?
2. Would building this cause me to delay a milestone by more than 1 week?
3. Is this a Pro/Team/Enterprise blocker (will I lose the deal without it)?

If the answer to #1 is no and #2 is yes, decline politely and log the request.

### 13.5 Month-by-Month Personal Priorities

| Month | Primary Focus | Secondary Focus |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Build MVP | Set up legal entity, accounting |
| 2 | Complete MVP, 10 alpha users | Build waitlist |
| 3 | Beta launch (PH) | Talk to 30 users personally |
| 4 | Fix top 3 pain points from beta | Content marketing started |
| 5 | Pro tier launch | First outreach to press |
| 6 | Optimize conversion funnel | Hire design contractor |
| 7 | Team tier engineering | First Team accounts onboarding |
| 8 | Team growth | Hire PT support contractor |
| 9 | Voice beta | First Enterprise conversations |
| 10 | Voice polish + enterprise prep | SOC 2 prep begins |
| 11 | Enterprise launch | First full-time hire interview |
| 12 | Scale + Year 2 planning | Fundraising decision |

### 13.6 Avoiding Founder Failure Modes

**Failure mode 1: Building without selling**
At least 5 customer conversations per week from Month 1. Talk to alpha users. Call beta users who churned. Talk to potential users who didn't sign up. Do this before writing code in the morning.

**Failure mode 2: Premature optimization**
Don't optimize infrastructure until you have 500+ users. Don't rewrite the codebase "the right way" until it's actually causing problems. Ship fast, fix what breaks.

**Failure mode 3: Pricing too low**
$29/month and $49/user/month feel uncomfortably high to a first-time founder. They are not. Test raising prices at Month 6. Most founders who raise prices find conversion rates don't drop.

**Failure mode 4: Over-dependence on one channel**
Never let a single channel (e.g., a Product Hunt spike) represent more than 40% of acquisition. Diversify early.

**Failure mode 5: Building the feature instead of solving the problem**
Users say "I want a voice export button." They mean "I waste 20 minutes extracting decisions from meeting recordings." Build the solution, not the literal request.

## 14. Fundraising vs. Bootstrapping Analysis

### 14.1 The Core Question

Gneva is bootstrappable to $80K MRR. This is a material fact that gives you genuine leverage: you don't need outside capital to survive. The question is not "do we need to raise?" but "would raising capital accelerate the outcome enough to justify the dilution and constraints?"

### 14.2 Bootstrapping Path

**Advantages:**
- Full ownership of outcome
- No investor pressure to optimize for growth over sustainability
- Faster decision-making (no board, no reporting)
- Can be profitable from Month 4; can pay yourself a real salary from Month 8
- Optionality: raise later from a position of strength, or don't raise at all

**Disadvantages:**
- Slower engineering velocity (can't hire quickly)
- Microsoft/Read.ai can outspend you on features
- Enterprise sales requires headcount you can't afford alone
- Geography and language expansion delayed
- Exit multiples for bootstrapped SaaS are lower than VC-backed (5–8× ARR vs. 15–25× ARR)

**Bootstrap scenario outcome:**
- Month 24: $280K MRR, $3.36M ARR
- Founder income: $150–200K/year
- Business value: $16–27M (5–8× ARR)
- This is a genuinely excellent outcome for a solo founder

### 14.3 Seed Fundraising Path

**When to raise:**
- **Don't raise before $10K MRR.** Pre-revenue or pre-PMF raises give away too much for too little.
- **Target $20–40K MRR as seed raise trigger.** At this point you have proof of growth, unit economics, and enough users for investor diligence.
- **Optimal raise window: Months 9–12.** Revenue trajectory is steep, voice feature launches make for a compelling story, Enterprise is just starting.

**Amount to raise:** $1–2M seed

**Use of funds:**
- Engineer hire #1 ($120K salary + equity): Months 13–14
- Engineer hire #2 ($120K): Month 16
- Sales/customer success hire ($90K): Month 14
- Marketing budget ($20K/month from Month 14)
- SOC 2 + legal compliance ($40K)
- 18-month runway at $200K burn = requires $3.6M, but revenue covers most costs

**Target investors:**
- Pre-seed / seed funds focused on productivity/future of work: Bessemer, Craft Ventures, Initialized, First Round
- Solo-founder-friendly funds: Indie.vc (now Earnest Capital), TinySeed
- Angels from the meeting intelligence / enterprise productivity space
- Strategic angels: former Zoom, Notion, Slack executives

**Realistic valuation at $30K MRR with 25%+ MoM growth:**
- Seed round: $8–12M pre-money valuation
- Raise $2M → founder owns ~75–80% post-money
- If Series A at $8M ARR → $50M+ pre-money → seed investors 4–6× return, founder has $37–40M stake

### 14.4 Decision Framework

Use this decision tree at Month 9–10:

```
Is MRR growing >20% MoM consistently?
├─ YES → Is the primary constraint capital or time?
│        ├─ TIME (can't hire fast enough) → Raise seed
│        └─ CAPITAL (can't buy ads, infrastructure) → Raise seed
│                  unless: bootstrap cash flow can cover in <3 months
└─ NO → Fix PMF first. Do NOT raise. Fundraise will distract you.
          Is churn >5%/month? → Fix product. Fundraise won't save you.
          Is CAC >$200? → Fix marketing. More budget won't help.
```

**Recommendation:** Bootstrap through Month 10. If MRR is at $30K+ and growing >20% MoM, raise a $1.5–2M seed with the following constraints:
- No more than 20% dilution
- No participating preferred
- 1× non-participating liquidation preference
- Board: founder controls; no investor board seat until Series A

### 14.5 Strategic Acquisition as Exit Path

Worth modeling from the start, even if not the plan:

**Most likely acquirers:**
- Notion ($10B valuation, needs meeting intelligence to compete with Microsoft)
- Zoom (strategic fit: native AI in meetings)
- Salesforce (meeting memory → CRM gold mine)
- Microsoft (if Copilot falls short; more likely they build than buy)
- Atlassian (Confluence + Jira + Gneva = complete organizational intelligence)

**Acquisition trigger:** $5–10M ARR, strong retention, proprietary memory graph

**Valuation range at acquisition:** 10–20× ARR = $50–200M at $5–10M ARR

This exit is realistic without raising venture capital. A $100M acqui-hire/acquisition of a profitable, solo-built SaaS business is a legitimate life-changing outcome.
